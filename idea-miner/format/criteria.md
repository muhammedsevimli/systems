# Scoring Rubric · Four Criteria

> Every idea is scored 1-5 on four criteria. A one-line reason under every score is required. Total out of 20.
> A score is never written without a reason. If the signal is weak, the score is low; the system does not do optimism.

## Criterion 1 · Market size (how many people live this pain, is money moving)
How many people have this pain, how often, and is there already money moving around it.
- **5:** a lot of people have it constantly, money already moves around the topic (people pay someone for this work, or burn time and money on it). The signal carries explicit money language.
- **4:** a wide audience, a regular pain, money visible indirectly.
- **3:** a specific niche has it, moderate frequency.
- **2:** a narrow niche, or a pain felt rarely.
- **1:** almost nobody is insisting on it; one person said it once.
> Reading: if there is a real signal saying "I would pay for this", the floor is 4. If there is only "I wish this existed", the floor is 3.

## Criterion 2 · Feasibility (can YOU build this, this week)
Read against your own building ability in `you/01-profile.md`. The question: can you get the first working version out in a reasonable time.
- **5:** simple data entry plus a panel or an automation; an MVP comes out this week with the structures you are comfortable with.
- **4:** a few moving parts, but all of them inside your comfort zone.
- **3:** one hard part (payments, an external integration), learnable.
- **2:** several hard parts, or a structure you said you avoid in your profile.
- **1:** needs a license or regulation (banking, medical diagnosis, legal), heavy infrastructure, or constant live operation.
> When you see a risky field (license, compliance, personal data), lower the score and add the note "this field needs permits and compliance". Do not quietly hand out a high score.

## Criterion 3 · Competition gap (is there room, can you differentiate)
What shape the existing solutions are in, and whether there is a gap you could walk into.
- **5:** there is no good solution, or the existing ones are expensive, complicated, or built for a different market; a clear gap.
- **4:** solutions exist, but a specific segment or feature is open.
- **3:** competition is crowded, but you can differentiate on one axis (price, language, niche).
- **2:** strong, cheap, entrenched players; differentiating is hard.
- **1:** a saturated market with nowhere to differentiate.
> Rule: if it resembles an existing product, do not propose a CLONE. On this line write "the existing solution is X, the gap is here" as the POINT OF DIFFERENCE. If there is no gap, the score is low.

## Criterion 4 · Market fit for your region (will it hold up where you sell, can you collect)
Will the idea work in the reality of the region and market written in `you/01-profile.md`: payment culture, language, local need, collection.
- **5:** a pain specific to your region (local rules, local language content, a local way of operating), payment is easy to collect there, the local audience is clear.
- **4:** it has a strong equivalent in your market, collection is solvable.
- **3:** a universal pain, valid in your market too, but no local advantage.
- **2:** the payment habit is weak in your market, or local fit is shaky.
- **1:** there is no equivalent in your market; it is specific to somewhere else.
> Reading: if the signal is in your customers' own language and describes a local way of working, the floor is 4. If there is a sign that "nobody here pays for this", lower the score.
> This criterion is a variable, not a constant. Score it against the region in `you/01-profile.md`, not against a market the system assumes.

## Total and thresholds
- Total = the sum of the four scores (between 4 and 20).
- **16-20:** a strong candidate, the front of the queue you could build this week.
- **12-15:** a good idea with one weak criterion; note how you would close that weakness.
- **8-11:** second tier; it rises if the signal strengthens or one part gets solved.
- **4-7:** eliminate or park it for now.
> On a tie: the idea with money language (criterion 1) and the one you can actually build (criterion 2) goes ahead.

## Signal strength (a confidence layer above the score)
As important as the score: how many independent signals back the idea.
- **Strong:** different people, in different sources, repeated the same pain. Money language present.
- **Medium:** a few signals, pointing the same way.
- **Weak:** one person, one place. It enters the report with a "weak signal, confirm this first" note and is not put at the top even with a high score.

## Honest limit (written on purpose)
- This system scans demand ITSELF (with Claude Code's web and shell tools). You are not pasting; you give the field you care about, and the system scans Hacker News (fully automatic), Reddit public RSS (fully automatic), and web and community forums (fully automatic) and collects the real demand sentences. X wants a login, so it is not scanned automatically; a one-click search link is produced for it (semi-automatic). Collecting, turning into ideas, scoring, and ranking are automatic.
- The system does NOT invent signals. If it cannot reach a source it says "could not be reached" and writes no sentence. If you have no signals, there are no ideas to score; it says "scan first".
- The scan is not infinitely wide. It is as wide as the search queries you give; good queries mean an accurate scan.
- Scores are a compass, not a fact. The more plentiful the signal and the clearer the money language, the more reliable the score. The decision (what you build) always stays with you.
